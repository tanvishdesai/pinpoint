## Category definitions

**Direct predecessors (target: 4–5 papers)**
Papers that do the closest thing to what your paper does. Your paper must acknowledge
and explicitly differentiate from every paper in this category. If a reviewer asks
"how is this different from [X]?" and [X] is not in this category, you have a problem.

**Methodological foundations (target: 4–5 papers)**
Papers that introduce the methods, architectures, loss functions, or evaluation
frameworks you use or build on. These are cited to show you understand the tools.
Examples: the original ResNet paper if you use ResNet, the original SHAP paper if
you apply SHAP, the paper that introduced your dataset.

**Gap-exposing papers (target: 3–5 papers)**
Papers whose limitations directly motivate why your work needs to exist. The gap you
identify in each of these is a building block of your problem statement. A paper can
be a gap-exposer even if it is high-quality — good papers still have scope limitations.
